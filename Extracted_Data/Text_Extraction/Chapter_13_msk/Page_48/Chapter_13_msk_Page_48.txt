Ewing sarcoma is a highly malignant small round cell tumor (similar to PNET) affecting
children and adolescents with a male predominance. The clinical presentation is nonspecific.
Ewing sarcoma usually presents with pain. Systemic symptoms including fever are often
present, making the distinction between Ewing sarcoma and osteomyelitis difficult.
Ewing sarcoma is the second most common pediatric primary bone tumor (following
osteosarcoma).
Radiographic features are of an aggressive lesion, with permeative bone destruction,
aggressive periosteal reaction, and often an associated soft-tissue mass.
In addition to Ewing sarcoma, the differential of an aggressive lytic lesion in a child includes
osteomyelitis, eosinophilic granuloma, and metastatic neuroblastoma.
